Interview with Eco Gear Maker

📅 December 10, 2024 👤 Mike Rodriguez 📖 12 min read 💬 8 comments
  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Eco Gear Maker Interview

Maria Santos never intended to become a gear manufacturer. A successful corporate lawyer specializing in environmental law, she was living a comfortable life in San Francisco when a life-changing backpacking trip through Southeast Asia opened her eyes to the plastic pollution crisis. Three years later, her company EcoVenture Gear is producing some of the most innovative sustainable travel equipment on the market.

I had the privilege of spending a day with Maria at her carbon-neutral manufacturing facility in Costa Rica, where she's not just making gear—she's reimagining what responsible manufacturing can look like in the 21st century.

From Courtroom to Workshop

Mike: Maria, thanks for taking the time today. Let's start at the beginning. How does a successful environmental lawyer end up manufacturing backpacks in Costa Rica?

Maria: Laughs It's definitely not the career path I envisioned! I was doing well as a lawyer, working on environmental regulations, but I felt like I was fighting problems after they'd already been created. The turning point came during a three-month sabbatical in 2021. I was backpacking through Vietnam, Thailand, and Cambodia, and I was horrified by the amount of plastic waste I saw—especially from travelers like myself.

I remember standing on a beautiful beach in Thailand that was literally covered in plastic bottles, food containers, and gear packaging. Most of it had tourist brand names on it. I realized I was part of the problem, not the solution.

Mike: That must have been a powerful moment. What happened next?

Maria: I spent the rest of that trip researching sustainable alternatives, and I was shocked by how limited the options were. Everything was either extremely expensive, had questionable sustainability claims, or simply didn't exist. When I got home, I couldn't stop thinking about it. I kept asking myself: what if someone could make truly sustainable gear that was also affordable and functional?

The Birth of EcoVenture Gear

Mike: So you decided to be that someone. But starting a manufacturing company is incredibly complex. How did you even begin?

Maria: Honestly, I had no idea what I was getting into! Laughs I spent six months just learning about textiles, manufacturing processes, and supply chains. I reached out to sustainability experts, gear designers, and manufacturers around the world. Most people told me it was impossible to make truly sustainable gear at an affordable price point.

But then I connected with Dr. Ana Gutierrez at the University of Costa Rica. She had been developing new materials from agricultural waste—specifically, creating strong fibers from discarded coffee cherry husks and banana plant stems. When I saw her lab work, I knew I had found my starting point.

Mike: That's fascinating. Is that how you ended up in Costa Rica?

Maria: Exactly. Costa Rica has an incredible commitment to sustainability—they're carbon negative as a country and have amazing renewable energy infrastructure. Plus, they have abundant agricultural waste that we could turn into materials. It seemed like the perfect place to prove that sustainable manufacturing could work.

Innovative Materials and Processes

Mike: Can you walk me through how you actually make a backpack from coffee waste? It sounds almost magical.

Maria: Leads me to the production floor It's actually quite ingenious once you understand the process. Coffee farmers normally throw away the cherry husks after extracting the beans, but these husks contain incredibly strong cellulose fibers. We collect them from local farms—which also provides farmers with an additional income stream—and process them through a mechanical separation technique that preserves the fiber strength.

We then blend these fibers with recycled plastic bottles to create a yarn that's stronger than conventional polyester but completely biodegradable at end of life. The fabric is then woven on solar-powered looms right here in our facility.

Mike: And the manufacturing process itself? I notice this doesn't look like a typical factory.

Maria: That's intentional. We designed this facility to be a closed-loop system. Our energy comes from solar panels and micro-hydro generators powered by the nearby stream. All our water is recycled through a natural filtration system. Even our dyes come from local plants and minerals.

Most importantly, we pay our workers a living wage—significantly above the local average—and provide healthcare, continuing education, and profit sharing. Sustainability has to include human sustainability, not just environmental.

Sustainable Manufacturing Highlights

  • Coffee cherry husks converted to strong fibers
  • Solar-powered looms and micro-hydro generators
  • Natural water filtration system
  • Plant-based dyes
  • Living wages and worker benefits
  • Closed-loop manufacturing process

Challenges of Sustainable Manufacturing

Mike: This all sounds incredible, but I imagine there are significant challenges. What's been the hardest part?

Maria: Sighs The biggest challenge is competing on price with conventional manufacturers who externalize their environmental and social costs. When a factory in another country can make a backpack for $15 by paying workers poverty wages and dumping chemicals into rivers, it's hard to compete with our $45 cost structure—even though our true cost to society is much lower.

We've also faced skepticism from retailers who assume sustainable means compromised performance. We've had to do extensive testing to prove that our gear is actually more durable than conventional alternatives.

Mike: How do you address the durability question?

Maria: Testing, testing, testing. We partner with outdoor gear testing labs and even send prototypes to extreme athletes and long-term travelers. Our current backpack design has been tested to withstand twice the stress cycles of industry standards. The agricultural fibers actually create a more flexible, tear-resistant fabric than pure synthetic materials.

We also offer a lifetime repair guarantee. If something breaks, we'll fix it for free. It's not just good customer service—it's part of our sustainability commitment. The most sustainable product is one that never needs to be replaced.

Beyond Products: Building a Movement

Mike: EcoVenture Gear seems to be about more than just selling backpacks. Can you tell me about your broader mission?

Maria: Absolutely. We see ourselves as proving that a different kind of business model is possible. We're completely transparent about our supply chain, our costs, and our environmental impact. We publish annual sustainability reports that include third-party audits of everything from our carbon footprint to our worker satisfaction surveys.

We also allocate 5% of our revenue to environmental restoration projects in the communities where we source materials. This year, we're funding reforestation efforts and supporting local farmers in transitioning to regenerative agriculture practices.

Mike: That's inspiring. What advice would you give to other entrepreneurs who want to start sustainable businesses?

Maria: First, be prepared for it to be harder and take longer than you think. Sustainable manufacturing requires rethinking everything from materials to logistics to business models. You can't just swap in a few eco-friendly components and call it sustainable.

Second, find partners who share your values. Our success has been built on relationships with like-minded suppliers, researchers, and customers who understand that true cost includes environmental and social impact.

Finally, be transparent about your limitations. We're not perfect—no company is. But we're committed to continuous improvement and honest communication about our progress and challenges.

About EcoVenture Gear

  • Founded: 2022
  • Location: San José, Costa Rica
  • Employees: 45 (all paid living wage)
  • Materials: Coffee waste, banana fiber, recycled plastic
  • Certifications: B-Corp, Carbon Neutral, Fair Trade
  • Annual Impact: 50,000 lbs of agricultural waste diverted, 200 tons CO2 sequestered

Learn More: Visit EcoVenture Gear's website for detailed sustainability reports and product information. They also offer virtual factory tours for customers interested in seeing their processes firsthand.

Looking Ahead

As our conversation winds down, Maria shows me prototypes of their upcoming modular system—a backpack that can transform into a day pack, a laptop bag, or even a small travel organizer depending on your needs. The innovation is impressive, but what strikes me most is Maria's unwavering commitment to proving that business can be a force for environmental and social good.

"We're not trying to be the biggest gear company. We're trying to be the most regenerative. If we can prove this model works, maybe other companies will follow suit."

In an industry often criticized for greenwashing and fast fashion mentality, Maria Santos and EcoVenture Gear represent something different: a genuine commitment to creating products that heal rather than harm our planet. Whether you're in the market for new gear or not, their story offers hope that a more sustainable future isn't just possible—it's being built, one recycled coffee husk at a time.
